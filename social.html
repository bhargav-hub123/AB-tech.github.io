Geography Lesson-2    Physical Features Of India

Q1. What are tectonic plates?

Ans. The crust (upper part) of the earth has been formed out of seven major and some minor plates. These are called tectonic plates.

Q2. What is the Bhabar?

Ans. Bhabar is a narrow belt of the Ganga plain covered with pebbles lies along the foothills of Shiwaliks.

Q3. Name the three major divisions of the Himalaya from north to south.

Ans. (a)The Greater Himalayas or Himadri (Inner Himalayas)
(b)Himachal or Lesser Himalayas (Middle Himalayas)
(c)The Shiwaliks (Outer Himalayas)

Q4.Which plateau lies between the Aravali and the Vindhyan Ranges?

Ans. Malwa plateau lies between the Aravali and the Vindhyan Ranges.

Q5. Name the island group of India having coral origin.?

Ans. Lakshadweep islands are the island group of India having coral origin.

Q6. Distinguish between:
(i) Bhangar and Khadar
(ii) Western Ghats and Eastern Ghats

Ans(i). {Write in Tabular Form}
Bhangar

1. This is a highland composed of old alluvium.

2.It is always above the level of flood plain.

3. It is often saturated with calcareous nodules known as kankar.

4. This is not much suited for cultivation.


Khadar
1. This is lowland composed of new alluvium.

2. It is flooded almost every year and new alluvium is deposited.

3. It is often characterised by clay soil, which is very fertile.

4. It is ideal for intensive agriculture.


Ans(ii). {Write in Tabular Form}

Western Ghats

1. Western Ghats mark the western edge of the Deccan Plateau

2.They are continuous and can only be crossed through passes only, e.g., Pal Ghat, Bhor Ghat, etc.

3. They lie parallel to the Western coast along the Arabian sea.

4. They are higher in south and the height increases towards the south.

5. Average height is between 900 m to 1600 m.

6. The highest peak is Anai Mudi with height of 2,695m above the sea level.

Eastern Ghats
1. Eastern Ghats mark the eastern edge of the Deccan Plateau.

2. They are discontinuous and irregular and have been dissected by the rivers draining into the Bay of Bengal.

3. They lie along the eastern coast along the Bay of Bengal.

4. They are higher in the north and the height increases towards the north.

5. Average height is 600m.

6. The highest peak is Mahendragiri with height of 1,501m above the sea level.

Ans. The major physiographic divisions of India are:

History Lesson-1    The French Revolution

Q1. Describe the circumstances leading to the outbreak of revolutionary protest in France ?

ANS: The reasons for the outbreak of the French Revolution were

1. Louis XVI autocratic (dictator), led a luxurious life was inefficient and was a weak ruler.

2. The society was organised into three estates namely – the 1st estate, the 2nd estate & the third estate.

3. The members of the first two estates enjoyed all the privileges and the members of the third estate were burdened by taxes.

4. The treasury of the king was empty on account of the long wars.

5. Role of philosophers inspired the people. They played a vital role in exposing the evils of the old regime.

6. There was severe winter with bad harvest which increased the prices and shortage of bread.

7. All these led to the outbreak of revolt in France. The agitated crowd stormed and destroyed the Bastille.

Q2. Which group of French society benefitted from the revolution? Which group were forced to relinquish power? Which sections of society would have been disappointed with outcome of the revolution?

ANS: a) The members of third estate were benefitted. It included the new middle class, big business men, merchants, court officials and professionals like lawyers etc. They were given political powers.

b) The Nobility and clergy were forced to relinquish (give up) power. Now the French society was organised on the basis of social equality. The revolution also led to nationalisation of church property.

c) The poor sections of the society and women would have been disappointed.

Q3. Describe the legacy of the French Revolution for the people of the world during the nineteenth centuries.

Ans. 1. The ideas of liberty and democratic rights were the most important legacy of the French Revolution.

2. These ideas spread from France to the rest of the world where feudal system was finally abolished.

3. Colonised people reworked on the idea of freedom from colonial bondage.

4. The idea of Nationalism spread throughout the world. Now people began to question the absolute power.

5. The Constitution of 1791 began with declaration of Rights of Man and Citizens.

6. The impact of French Revolution would be seen in India too. Tipu Sultan and Raja Rammohan Roy got deeply influenced by the ideas of the Revolution.

In the end, we can say that after the French Revolution people all over the world became aware of their rights.

Q4. Draw up a list of democratic rights we enjoy today whose origin could be traced to the French Revolution.

ANS. Some of the rights that we enjoy today can be traced to the French Revolution are as follows-

i. Right to Equality including equality before law, prohibition of discrimination and equality of opportunity in matters of employment.

ii. Right to Freedom of Speech and Expression including right to practice any profession or occupation.

iii. Right against Exploitation.

iv. Right to life.

v. Right to Vote.

Q5. Would you agree with the view that the message of universal rights was beset with contradictions? Explain.

Ans. Yes, the message of universal rights was beset with contradictions.

1. The French Revolution could not bring economic equality. Unless there is economic equality, real equality cannot be received in any sphere.

2. The right to vote and elect their representatives did not solve the poor man’s problems.

3. Women did not enjoy political rights and hold political offices like men.

4. France continued to hold and expand colonies. Thus, its image as a liberator could not last for long time.

5. Slave trade, one of the most inhuman practise was another contradiction.

Q6. How would you explain the rise of Napoleon?

Ans. 1. The political instability of the Directory paved the way to the rise of Napoleon.

2. In 1804, Napoleon Bonaparte crowned himself Emperor of France. He set out to conquer neighbouring European countries, dispossessing dynasties and creating kingdoms where he placed members of his family.

3. Napoleon viewed himself as a moderniser of Europe. He introduced many laws such as the protection of private property and a uniform system of weights and measures provided by the decimal system.

4. Initially, many saw Napoleon as a liberator who would bring freedom for the people. But soon the Napoleon armies came to be viewed everywhere as an invading force.

5. But his rise to power did not last long. He was defeated at Waterloo in 1815.

Q7. Mention the reforms introduced by Robespierre.

ANS. The reforms introduced by Robespierre are –

1. Robespierre’s government issued laws placing a maximum ceiling on wages and prices.

2. Meat and bread were rationed.

3. Peasants were forced to transport their grain to the cities and sell it prices fixed by the government.

4. The use of more expensive white flour was forbidden.

5. Equality was also sought to be practised through forms of speech and address.

6. Churches were shut down and their buildings were converted into barracks or offices.

Q8. “The period between 1793 to1794 is referred as the Reign of Terror”. Explain.

ANS. The period between1793 to 1794 is referred as the Reign of Terror due to the following reasons-

1. Robespierre followed a policy of severe control and punishment.

2. All those whom he saw as being ‘enemies’ of the republic – ex. Nobles and clergy, members of other political parties, even members of his own party who did not agree with his methods – were arrested, imprisoned and tried by a revolutionary tribunal.

3. If the court found them guilty they were guillotined.

Q9. Describe the main effects of abolition of Censorship.

ANS. The main effects of abolition of Censorship were-

1. In the Old Regime all written material and cultural activities – books, newspaper, plays – could be published or performed only after they had been approved by the censors of the king.

2. Now the Declaration of the Rights of Man and the Citizen proclaimed freedom of speech and expression to be a natural right.

3. Newspapers, pamphlets, books and printed pictures flooded the towns of France from where they travelled rapidly into the countryside.

4. They all described and discussed the events and the changes taking place in France.

5. Thus making people aware of their rights.

Q10. Describe the organisation of the French Society in the 18th century (Old Regime).

Ans. The French society in the 18th century was divided into three estates.

• 1st Estate – Clergy. They enjoyed certain privileges by birth. The most important of these privileges was exemption from paying taxes.

• 2nd Estate – Nobility. The nobles and other rich people were also exempted from paying taxes. They also enjoyed feudal privileges like collection of feudal dues from the peasants.

• 3rd Estate – Big businessmen, merchants, court officials, lawyers, peasants, artisans, landless labourers and servants. Peasants made up of 90% of the population, but only a small number of them owned the lands which they cultivated.60% of the land was owned by the nobles and the church. The church also extracted taxes from the peasants. The peasants were obliged to render services to the lords like working in their fields and house, in the army etc. The burden of financing activities of the state through taxes was borne by the third estates only.

Q11. Which three causes led to Subsistence crisis in France during Old Regime?

ANS. The following are the causes that led to subsistence crisis in France during Old Regime-

• The population of France increased from 23 million in 1715 to 28 million in 1789. This led to the increase in the demand for food grains.

• When the production of food grains could not keep pace with the growing demand, the price of bread which was the staple food increased rapidly.

• On the other hand, the wages could not keep pace with the rise in prices. At the time of drought or hail, harvest reduced and things worsened. Thus, the gap between the poor and the rich widened and this led to the subsistence crisis.

Economics Lesson-1    The Story Of The Village Palampur

1. Modern farming methods require more inputs which are manufactured in Industry. Do you agree?

Ans) Yes, it is true that modern farming methods require more inputs than traditional farming. It requires inputs like Chemical fertilizers, Pesticides, Pump sets and Water supply. Most of these are manufactured in industries. Similarly, water supply is provided by Canals and Tanks.

2. How did spread of electricity help farmers of Palampur?

Ans) The spread of electricity helped the farmers of Palampur in the following ways-

==> Most of the houses have electricity connections.

==> It is used to run the tube wells in the fields.

==> It is used in various types of small businesses.

3. Construct a table on land among the 450 families in Palampur.

Ans) {Write in Table Form}

4. Why are wages of farm labourers less than minimum wages in Palampur?

Ans) The wages of farm labourers in Palampur are less than minimum wages because:

==> There is heavy competition for work among the farmers.

==> Employment is less and farmers are more and, therefore, farmers have to be content with whatever they are earning.

==> Land is owned by landlords who desire to earn more and more money by giving minimum wages.

==> The farmers are illiterate and unaware of the amount of minimum wages set by government.

5. What are the different ways of increasing production on the same piece of land?

Ans) The land is under cultivation is practically fixed, so to increase the production form a same piece of land, we can use the following methods-

==> Multiple Cropping- It is most common way of increasing production on a given piece of land. It means when two or more crops are grown on the same piece of land during a year, i.e. farmers should grow at least two main crops in a year. In India, some farmers are growing third crops also over the last 20 years such as in Palampur, Jowar and bajra are grown and potato is the third crop.

==> Modern Farming- Production on the same piece of land can also be increased by adopting modern methods of farming. The green revolution in India is a remarkable example of it. Under modern farming more cultivable area should be brought under high yielding varieties of seeds and irrigation. The use of farm machinery such as tractors, threshers and harvesters, makes cultivation faster and also help in increasing yield per hectare.

6. How do medium and large farmers obtain capital for farming? How is it different from small farmers?

Ans)

==> All farmers require capital during production. They require field as well as working capital.

==> Medium and large farmers have their own savings from farming.

==> They are able to arrange for their own capital. They sell their surplus in the market. A part of this is saved and the rest is used to buy machinery and other raw materials or lend to small farmers to make more profit.

==> Whereas the small farmers have to borrow money to arrange for capital. They borrow from large farmers, money lenders and traders. They are charged very high interest.

7. Describe the work of a farmer with 1 hectare of land?

Ans) A farmer who works on one hectare of land is called a small farmer. He carries out the following activities-

==> Ploughs the field by bullocks.

==> Sows the seed by simply sprinkling with hands.

==> Waters the field with the help of Persian wheel.

==> Sprays the pesticides by manual pumps.

==> Cuts the crops by hand operated tools.

8. What can be done so that non farming activities can be started in village?

Ans) Supply of electricity in villages needs to be improved. This will encourage the establishment of small scale industries.

==> More villages should be connected to the towns and cities through all weather road transport and telephonic system.

==> The villages should have markets where goods and services produced can be sold such as in Palampur, neighboring towns and cities provide market for milk, jaggery and wheat.

9. Name the non-farming activities in Palampur.

Ans) The non-farming activities in Palampur are-

==> Dairy farming

==> Small scale manufacturing

==> Transporting

==> Shop keeping

10. What is the aim of production? State any four requirements of production.

Ans) The aim of production is to generate income for those people who are involved in the production of goods and services.

1) Land- It is a passive factor of production. It requires human effort to make it a productive resource. No activity can take place in the absence of land and it is the basis of all human activities.

2)Labour- The second requirement is labour, i.e., people who will do the work. Some activities require educated and skilled labour to perform the necessary tasks and others require the workers who can do the manual work.

3)Physical capital- The third requirement is physical capital, i.e., the variety of inputs like machines, tools, raw materials and money are required at each stage of production. It is further divided into fixed capital and working capital.

4)Human Capital-It is the knowledge and enterprise to be able to put together land, labour and physical capital to produce an output either to use for themselves or to sell in the market.

Economics Lesson-2    People as a Resource

Q1: What do you understand by “PEOPLE AS RESOURCE”?

ANSWER:

“People as a resource” are a way of referring to a country’s working people in terms of their existing productive skills and abilities.

Q2: How is human resource different from other resources like land and physical capital?

ANSWER:

Human resource is different in the following ways:-

==> Land and other resources are fixed, limited and specified whereas human resources can be nurtured through education and health.

==> Human resources can bring a change in other resources whereas other resources cannot change or affect human resource.

==> Human resources can make use of land and physical capital whereas land and physical capital cannot become useful on its own.

Q3: What is the role of education in human capital formation?

ANSWER:

The role of education in human capital formation is as follows:-

==> Educated people can earn more than the uneducated people.

==> Literate population is an asset to an economy.

==> It leads to higher productivity.

==> It provides new aspirants and develops value of life.

==> It contributes to the growth of society.

==> It enhances the national income, cultural richness and the efficiency of the governance.

Q4: What is the role of health in human capital formation?

ANSWER:

The role of health in human capital formation is as follows:-

==> Healthier people have higher productivity because the health of a person helps him to realize his potential and the ability to fight illness. On the other hand, an unhealthy person becomes a liability for an organization.

==> It improves the quality of life. A healthy person is able to do his work in a proper and efficient way.

==> A healthy person makes greater contribution to society as compared to an unhealthy person.

==> Good health enables a person to earn more and to be more regular in his work.

Q5: What are the various activities undertaken in the primary sector, secondary sector and tertiary sector?

ANSWER:

A)Primary sector:- Agriculture, Forestry, Animal Husbandry, Fishing, Poultry Farming and Mining.

B)Secondary sector:-Manufacturing and Construction.

C)Tertiary sector:-Trade, Transport, Communication, Banking, Education, Health, Tourism Services, Insurance, etc.

Q6: What is the difference between economic activities and non-economic activities?

ANSWER:-

A)Economic activities:-

==> It refers to a human activity related to production and consumption of goods and services for economic gain.

==> These activities add value to the national income.

==> These are measured in monetary terms.

B)Non-Economic activities:-

==> It is an activity performed gladly, with the aim of providing services to others without any regard to monetary gain.

==> These activities do not add any value to the national income, as these are only for self consumption.

==> These are not measured in monetary terms.

Q7: Why are women employed in low paid work?

ANSWER:-

Education and skill are two determinants of the earning of any individual in the market. The women work in places where there is no job security. The various activities relating to legal protection meager. The majority of women have less education and low skill formation. All these factors lead to their irregular and low income. So, they are paid less as compared to men.

Q8: How will you explain the term “Unemployment “?

ANSWER:-

The term “Unemployment” means a situation where a section of people, who are able and willing to work do not find any gainful work to do.

Q9: What is the difference between “Disguised Unemployment” and “Seasonal Unemployment”?

ANSEWR:-

A)Disguised unemployment:- In case of disguised unemployment, people appear to be employed but they are actually unemployed. For example:- Sometimes in agriculture families eight people are working on a farm, whereas only four people are needed to do that work. Thus, four people are surplus and they are not needed on farm. They also do not help to increase the production. If these four people are removed from the farm, the production from the farm will not decrease. Therefore, these four people appear to be employed but are actually disguisedly unemployed.

B)Seasonal unemployment:- Seasonal unemployment happens when people are not able to find jobs during some months of the year. People dependent on agriculture usually face such problem. There are certain busy seasons when Sowing, Harvesting, Weeding and Threshing are done. When the plants are growing , there is not much work. During this period, they remain unemployed and said to be seasonally unemployed.

Q10: Which capital would you consider the best Land, Labour, Physical capital and Human capital?

ANSWER:-

Human capital is the best capital because it can make use of Land, Labour and Physical capital. The other factors cannot become useful on their own. The knowledge expertise in a human enables him to develop Land, Labour and Physical capital to produce an output for his own use or to sell in the market.

Q11: Describe five main features of “Sarva Shiksha Abhiyan”.

ANSWER:-

The five main features of “Sarva Shiksha Abhiyan” are:-

==> It is a significant step towards providing elementary education to all children in the age group of 6-14 years.

==> It is a time bound initiative of the Central Government, in partnership with the states, the local Government and the community for attaining the goal of universalization of elementary education.

==> In this, bridge courses and back-to-school campus have been introduced to increase the enrolment in elementary education.

==> Mid –day meal scheme has been introduced to encourage attendance in schools and increase their nutritional status.

==> These types of policies of the Government could add to the literate minds and further the economic development.

Q12: What part does health play in the individual’s working life ?

ANSWER:-

Health plays a very important role in an individual’s life because as we all know “Health is Wealth” and only a health person can work or perform to his full potential. An unhealthy person cannot work efficiently. A healthy person is able to work hard and better. Thus, earning more and living a better life. If the body is healthy, only then one can perform well. So, we can say that heath plays a vital role in an individual’s life.

Q13: Why is education unemployment a peculiar problem of India?

ANSWER:-

Education unemployment is a peculiar problem of India because they are not able to find jobs for themselves. This is a situation where unemployment of graduates and post-graduates has increased faster than among the matriculates. A paradoxical situation is witnessed as there is surplus manpower in certain categories with shortage of manpower in other. There is unemployment among technically qualified persons on one hand, while there are categories of technical skills requires for economic growth. Thus, it is a peculiar problem in India.

Q14: In which field do you think, India can build the maximum employment opportunities?

ANSWER:-

There is no further scope in the Primary sector because there is already disguised unemployment. In the Secondary sector, small scale manufacturing is the most labour absorbing sector. In case of Tertiary sector, various new services like bio- technology and information technology are coming up and there is scope for further employment opportunities.

Q15: Can you suggest some measures in the education system to mitigate the problem of the educated unemployment?

ANSWER:-

Some of the measures in the education system to mitigate the problem of the educated are:-

==> Vocational education should be encouraged so that people do not have difficulty in getting jobs because they will be better trained for work.

==> More use of information technology should be made in giving education.

==> Education should be job-oriented.

==> More employment opportunities should be made available to the educated people in the Tertiary sector.

Q16: Can you imagine some village which initially had no job opportunities but later came up with many?

ANSWER:-

==> Rampur was the village which initially depended on agriculture and on rainfall. Then, when electricity was provided, the people started using electricity to irrigate their fields and grow 2-3 crops in a year to get more work.

==> Some started small scale industries based on the use of electricity and provided employment to people. A school was established so that the people became educated and started getting employment in and outside the village.

==> The village became prosperous and soon had better health, Education, Transport and job facilities.
