History Lesson-1    The French Revolution

Q1. Describe the circumstances leading to the outbreak of revolutionary protest in France ?

ANS: The reasons for the outbreak of the French Revolution were

1. Louis XVI autocratic (dictator), led a luxurious life was inefficient and was a weak ruler.

2. The society was organised into three estates namely – the 1st estate, the 2nd estate & the third estate.

3. The members of the first two estates enjoyed all the privileges and the members of the third estate were burdened by taxes.

4. The treasury of the king was empty on account of the long wars.

5. Role of philosophers inspired the people. They played a vital role in exposing the evils of the old regime.

6. There was severe winter with bad harvest which increased the prices and shortage of bread.

7. All these led to the outbreak of revolt in France. The agitated crowd stormed and destroyed the Bastille.

Q2. Which group of French society benefitted from the revolution? Which group were forced to relinquish power? Which sections of society would have been disappointed with outcome of the revolution?

ANS: a) The members of third estate were benefitted. It included the new middle class, big business men, merchants, court officials and professionals like lawyers etc. They were given political powers.

b) The Nobility and clergy were forced to relinquish (give up) power. Now the French society was organised on the basis of social equality. The revolution also led to nationalisation of church property.

c) The poor sections of the society and women would have been disappointed.

Q3. Describe the legacy of the French Revolution for the people of the world during the nineteenth centuries.

Ans. 1. The ideas of liberty and democratic rights were the most important legacy of the French Revolution.

2. These ideas spread from France to the rest of the world where feudal system was finally abolished.

3. Colonised people reworked on the idea of freedom from colonial bondage.

4. The idea of Nationalism spread throughout the world. Now people began to question the absolute power.

5. The Constitution of 1791 began with declaration of Rights of Man and Citizens.

6. The impact of French Revolution would be seen in India too. Tipu Sultan and Raja Rammohan Roy got deeply influenced by the ideas of the Revolution.

In the end, we can say that after the French Revolution people all over the world became aware of their rights.

Q4. Draw up a list of democratic rights we enjoy today whose origin could be traced to the French Revolution.

ANS. Some of the rights that we enjoy today can be traced to the French Revolution are as follows-

i. Right to Equality including equality before law, prohibition of discrimination and equality of opportunity in matters of employment.

ii. Right to Freedom of Speech and Expression including right to practice any profession or occupation.

iii. Right against Exploitation.

iv. Right to life.

v. Right to Vote.

Q5. Would you agree with the view that the message of universal rights was beset with contradictions? Explain.

Ans. Yes, the message of universal rights was beset with contradictions.

1. The French Revolution could not bring economic equality. Unless there is economic equality, real equality cannot be received in any sphere.

2. The right to vote and elect their representatives did not solve the poor man’s problems.

3. Women did not enjoy political rights and hold political offices like men.

4. France continued to hold and expand colonies. Thus, its image as a liberator could not last for long time.

5. Slave trade, one of the most inhuman practise was another contradiction.

Q6. How would you explain the rise of Napoleon?

Ans. 1. The political instability of the Directory paved the way to the rise of Napoleon.

2. In 1804, Napoleon Bonaparte crowned himself Emperor of France. He set out to conquer neighbouring European countries, dispossessing dynasties and creating kingdoms where he placed members of his family.

3. Napoleon viewed himself as a moderniser of Europe. He introduced many laws such as the protection of private property and a uniform system of weights and measures provided by the decimal system.

4. Initially, many saw Napoleon as a liberator who would bring freedom for the people. But soon the Napoleon armies came to be viewed everywhere as an invading force.

5. But his rise to power did not last long. He was defeated at Waterloo in 1815.

Q7. Mention the reforms introduced by Robespierre.

ANS. The reforms introduced by Robespierre are –

1. Robespierre’s government issued laws placing a maximum ceiling on wages and prices.

2. Meat and bread were rationed.

3. Peasants were forced to transport their grain to the cities and sell it prices fixed by the government.

4. The use of more expensive white flour was forbidden.

5. Equality was also sought to be practised through forms of speech and address.

6. Churches were shut down and their buildings were converted into barracks or offices.

Q8. “The period between 1793 to1794 is referred as the Reign of Terror”. Explain.

ANS. The period between1793 to 1794 is referred as the Reign of Terror due to the following reasons-

1. Robespierre followed a policy of severe control and punishment.

2. All those whom he saw as being ‘enemies’ of the republic – ex. Nobles and clergy, members of other political parties, even members of his own party who did not agree with his methods – were arrested, imprisoned and tried by a revolutionary tribunal.

3. If the court found them guilty they were guillotined.

Q9. Describe the main effects of abolition of Censorship.

ANS. The main effects of abolition of Censorship were-

1. In the Old Regime all written material and cultural activities – books, newspaper, plays – could be published or performed only after they had been approved by the censors of the king.

2. Now the Declaration of the Rights of Man and the Citizen proclaimed freedom of speech and expression to be a natural right.

3. Newspapers, pamphlets, books and printed pictures flooded the towns of France from where they travelled rapidly into the countryside.

4. They all described and discussed the events and the changes taking place in France.

5. Thus making people aware of their rights.

Q10. Describe the organisation of the French Society in the 18th century (Old Regime).

Ans. The French society in the 18th century was divided into three estates.

• 1st Estate – Clergy. They enjoyed certain privileges by birth. The most important of these privileges was exemption from paying taxes.

• 2nd Estate – Nobility. The nobles and other rich people were also exempted from paying taxes. They also enjoyed feudal privileges like collection of feudal dues from the peasants.

• 3rd Estate – Big businessmen, merchants, court officials, lawyers, peasants, artisans, landless labourers and servants. Peasants made up of 90% of the population, but only a small number of them owned the lands which they cultivated.60% of the land was owned by the nobles and the church. The church also extracted taxes from the peasants. The peasants were obliged to render services to the lords like working in their fields and house, in the army etc. The burden of financing activities of the state through taxes was borne by the third estates only.

Q11. Which three causes led to Subsistence crisis in France during Old Regime?

ANS. The following are the causes that led to subsistence crisis in France during Old Regime-

• The population of France increased from 23 million in 1715 to 28 million in 1789. This led to the increase in the demand for food grains.

• When the production of food grains could not keep pace with the growing demand, the price of bread which was the staple food increased rapidly.

• On the other hand, the wages could not keep pace with the rise in prices. At the time of drought or hail, harvest reduced and things worsened. Thus, the gap between the poor and the rich widened and this led to the subsistence crisis.
